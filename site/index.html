Transit 2012
2012-06-06 // SOCIAL MEDIA SCIENCE

Join us in re-enacting one of the great scientific endeavours of the 18th century. Like Captain Cook before us, we will observe the transit of Venus to determine the distance between the Earth and the Sun - the Astronomical Unit.

To participate make one or both of the following observations. Be sure to convert the times you record to Universal Time (which is often referred to as GMT).

Record the time when Venus has just fully entered the Sun's disc, and tweet it (in UTC):
#venusobs entered 7:39:21
Record the time when Venus is just about to exit the Sun's disc, and tweet it (in UTC):
#venusobs left 13:03:12

Be sure to geotag your tweets, so we know where you are — it's important for the maths.

A phone app is available from transitofvenus.nl which will allow you to train yourself to accurately observe these two events.

To achieve an accurate observation, we recommend using a pinhole or telescopic projection. For more information about the transit and how to observe it, see Transit of Venus Australia - this site includes approximate transit times, so you'll know when to set up and observe.

Warning: Do not attempt to observe the sun directly; doing so will cause serious and permanent damage to your eyesight. These safety steps from the Queensland Government should be followed.

How it works

All of the tweeted observations are logged by a server, which stores them in a database. The server periodically takes every pair of observations (that has greater than five degrees of latitude between them) and applies Delisle's method to calculate the AU. All of these results are then averaged, to produce the number that you will see updating in real time as observations come in.
